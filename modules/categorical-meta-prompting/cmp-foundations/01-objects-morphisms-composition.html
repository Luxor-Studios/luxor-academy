01Primer

The three things every category has

A category is specified by three pieces of data: objects, morphisms (arrows) between objects, and a composition operation that combines two compatible arrows into a third. Add two laws — identity (every object has an arrow to itself that does nothing) and associativity ((h ∘ g) ∘ f = h ∘ (g ∘ f)) — and you have a category.

The CATEGORICAL-ONTOLOGY.md document in categorical-meta-prompting fixes the ontology the rest of the framework depends on: the objects of the meta-prompting category are Task, Prompt, Context, and Quality ∈ [0,1]; the morphisms are the composition operators → (sequence), || (parallel), ⊗ (tensor), and >=> (Kleisli); and composition is explicitly tracked through a quality lattice where quality(A ⊗ B) ≤ min(q(A), q(B)) and quality(A || B) = mean(q(A), q(B)).

Why this matters: once you agree that prompts live in a category, you are no longer stringing together strings. You are composing typed arrows, and the composition laws — identity and associativity — give you predictable behaviour. F(id_task) = id_prompt means wrapping a no-op task yields a no-op prompt; F(g ∘ f) = F(g) ∘ F(f) means composing two task transformations equals transforming each separately and composing the results. Those two lines are the promise that categorical meta-prompting makes to you, and everything else in this Quest is earning it.

Objects, morphisms, and composition — plus identity and associativity — are the minimal vocabulary every category requires. Meta-prompting was missing exactly that vocabulary.

05Self-Check

1 — A category is specified by objects, morphisms, and composition. Which two laws must composition satisfy for the specification to actually be a category?
Every category requires (a) identity — for each object X there is an identity morphism id_X such that id_X ∘ f = f and g ∘ id_X = g for any compatible f, g — and (b) associativity: (h ∘ g) ∘ f = h ∘ (g ∘ f). These are the two axioms stated in docs/CATEGORICAL-ONTOLOGY.md §2.1 and enforced by the framework as F(id) = id and F(g ∘ f) = F(g) ∘ F(f).
2 — In the categorical-meta-prompting ontology, the → (sequence) operator and the ⊗ (tensor) operator share the same quality rule. What is it?
Both use min(q₁, q₂). docs/CATEGORICAL-ONTOLOGY.md §3.1 states: → (SEQUENCE) Quality: min(qₐ, qᵦ) and ⊗ (TENSOR) Quality: min(qₐ, qᵦ). The intuition: a chain or a tensor is only as good as its weakest link. Parallel (||) is the one that averages; Kleisli (>=>) is the one that converges iteratively.
3 — Why does the framework choose objects like Task, Prompt, Context, Quality rather than just String?
Categories only work when morphisms are typed. If every object is String, then every arrow is String → String and composition is trivial but meaningless. Distinct object types (Task, Prompt, Context) make F: Task → Prompt a real structural claim, not a rename — and they are what let the identity and composition laws carry content.
