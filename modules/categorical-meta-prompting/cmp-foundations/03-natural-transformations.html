Natural Transformations

01Primer

A natural transformation is a strategy swap that commutes

When you switch from zero-shot prompting to chain-of-thought, you are not rewriting every call site — you are replacing one functor with another. In categorical language, the thing that lets you do this consistently is a natural transformation α: F ⇒ G, a family of arrows α_X : F(X) → G(X) indexed by objects X, required to satisfy one square: for every morphism f: A → B, the diagram α_B ∘ F(f) = G(f) ∘ α_A must commute.

The categorical-meta-prompting framework enumerates the useful prompt-strategy functors — F_ZS (zero-shot, q ≈ 0.65), F_FS (few-shot, q ≈ 0.78), F_CoT (chain-of-thought, q ≈ 0.85), F_ToT (tree-of-thought, q ≈ 0.88), F_Meta (meta-prompting, q ≈ 0.90) — and defines transformations between them: α[ZS→CoT] literally adds the string "step by step"; α[FS→CoT] rewrites examples as reasoning traces; α[CoT→ToT] introduces branching and backtracking.

The naturality square is not bureaucracy; it is the correctness condition. It says: if you have a pipeline A → B under strategy F, and you want to flip the whole pipeline to strategy G, you can either (a) transform every stage and then run the new pipeline, or (b) run the old pipeline and transform the result — and the naturality law guarantees those two paths produce the same output. That is exactly the property that lets /transform @from:zero-shot @to:chain-of-thought swap strategies without touching the call sites. It is the categorical version of a plugin architecture, and the law is the contract that says the plugin will not silently change semantics.

A natural transformation α: F ⇒ G swaps an entire prompting strategy under the hood. The naturality square α_B ∘ F(f) = G(f) ∘ α_A is the contract that guarantees the swap does not break composition.

05Self-Check

1 — Write out the naturality condition that a transformation α: F ⇒ G between two functors must satisfy, for every morphism f: A → B.
The condition is α_B ∘ F(f) = G(f) ∘ α_A. This says the square with corners F(A), F(B), G(A), G(B) commutes: transforming after applying F equals applying G after transforming. Options mixing up the subscripts (e.g. α_A ∘ F(f) = G(f) ∘ α_B) would not type-check — F(f) lands in F(B), so it must compose with α_B, not α_A.
2 — According to the framework's strategy table, which transformation has the literal rule 'ADD "step by step"' attached to it?
α[ZS→CoT] is defined as ADD "step by step", upgrading zero-shot to chain-of-thought by prepending the reasoning instruction. The other listed transformations are α[FS→CoT] (rewrite examples as reasoning traces), α[CoT→ToT] (add branching + backtracking), and α[*→Meta] (wrap with a strategy-selection layer). See docs/CATEGORICAL-ONTOLOGY.md §2.4 TRANSFORMATION RULES.
3 — Why does the naturality square let you swap strategies 'without touching the call sites'?
The commuting square guarantees that (apply α at every stage, then run under G) produces the same result as (run under F, then apply α at the end). That interchange is exactly what 'hot-swap the whole pipeline' requires — you can replace F with G at any layer and the downstream composition continues to make sense. Without the naturality law, swapping F for G mid-pipeline could silently change semantics. (Naturality does NOT imply α is invertible, and it does NOT imply G has higher quality — though F_Meta does happen to be higher-quality than F_ZS.)
