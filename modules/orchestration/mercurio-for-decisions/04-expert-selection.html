01Primer

Three to seven diverse agents, weighted by domain

The Physical Plane Playbook is where MERCURIO's operational grounding lives — the sub-spec that translates intellectual understanding into coordinated work. Its core question, stated at the top of the playbook, is: Can we actually do this? What does execution require? How do we coordinate across domains? That question governs expert selection, because a panel is a micro-project.

The playbook gives expert selection a hard constraint that most teams ignore: coordination overhead scales faster than panel size. The exact formula from the Key Concepts section is N(N-1)/2 potential communication channels for N teams — 1 team has 0 channels, 2 teams have 1, 3 teams have 3, and 7 teams have 21. That is why MERCURIO caps panels at 3–7 experts. Below 3, you do not have enough diverse views to converge on anything robust. Above 7, coordination cost eats the convergence signal before it can crystallize.

Resource Planning (Method 4) gives you the procedure. List the tasks the panel must cover, identify skills needed per task, map against available experts, and find the bottleneck resource. A panel where two experts both cover the same dimension is a panel with a silent over-allocation. A panel missing a dimension is a panel where the missing plane will retaliate downstream. The Feasibility Assessment (Method 1) is the gate before the panel even meets: distinguish fixed from negotiable constraints, note hidden dependencies, and refuse to assemble a panel whose decision has already been pre-constrained.

In this module you read the Resource Matrix template literally from the playbook, then assemble a 3–7 expert panel for a sample decision — weighting each expert's vote by their domain coverage and checking coordination cost before you commit.

Coordination cost is N(N-1)/2. At 8 experts you pay 28 channels of overhead. That is why MERCURIO caps panels at 3-7: below 3 is under-diverse, above 7 the coordination cost eats the convergence signal.

### 5. Coordination Overhead

More teams = more coordination needed

```
1 team: No coordination needed
2 teams: One handoff point
3 teams: Three handoff points
N teams: N(N-1)/2 potential communication channels
```

The Physical Plane accounts for coordination cost.

### 6. The Buffer Principle

Always build in buffers for:
- **Time**: Unexpected obstacles always occur
- **People**: Someone will get sick, leave, have conflicting priorities
- **Money**: Scope creep and surprises cost more than expected
- **Knowledge**: You won't know everything needed upfront

05Self-Check

1 — The Physical Plane Playbook gives an exact formula for communication channels as team count N grows. How many channels exist for a panel of 7 experts?
N(N-1)/2 = 7*6/2 = 21 channels. The formula is why panels above 7 pay prohibitive coordination cost. At 8 you jump to 28, at 10 to 45. MERCURIO caps panels at 7 precisely because 21 is already near the ceiling of what a single synthesizer can weave coherently.
2 — You are assembling a panel to evaluate a new product feature. The Physical Plane Playbook's Resource Planning method (Method 4) asks you to do which of these FIRST?
Method 4 step 1: "List all tasks (from work breakdown)". Skills and people come after. Starting with experts before tasks is how panels over-index on whoever is loudest; starting with tasks is how MERCURIO ensures every dimension has coverage.
3 — Your panel has an architect, a security engineer, a DBA, and a backend engineer. Per the Physical Plane's integration guidance (Connection to Other Planes), what is this panel most likely to miss?
All four experts live on the Physical/Mental planes. The panel has no Spiritual Plane coverage — no ethics, values, or stakeholder-impact voice. The playbook states explicitly: "Executing work that's technically sound but ethically problematic" is the failure mode when Spiritual is absent. Add an ethics or user-advocate expert.
