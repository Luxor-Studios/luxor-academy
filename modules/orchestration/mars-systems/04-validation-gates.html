01Primer

Quality assurance is a running check, not a final audit

MARS the ARCHITECT is not done when the diagram is done. The agent definition builds a five-gate quality-assurance routine directly into the design posture — five continuous checks that fire during the architectural pass, not at the end of it. If any gate fails, the ARCHITECT self-corrects before the blueprint moves forward.

The five gates are explicit. Gate 1: Regularly verify that the design actually enables stated capabilities. That is the capability check — does the thing you drew actually do what you claimed? Gate 2: Check that integration remains coherent as complexity grows. Integration does not stay coherent for free; it has to be re-checked every time the system gets bigger. Gate 3: Ensure structural choices still serve transformation goals. Structure drifts — a choice that served the goal last week may be fighting it now. Gate 4: Identify and address single points of failure or brittleness. Gate 5 is the meta-gate: Self-correct when you notice emerging fragility or misalignment.

The fifth gate is what makes the first four operational. A validation gate that only names fragility is a memo. A validation gate that self-corrects is a feedback loop. This is where MARS the ARCHITECT stops being a diagramming agent and starts being a system that keeps its own designs honest.

The ship-or-fail decision is not a single audit at the end. It is the accumulated state of these five gates across the design pass. If any one of them is failing when the blueprint lands, the blueprint does not ship — it gets returned to the architect with a specific gate to re-run.

The five architect gates — capability, integration coherence, transformation alignment, single-point brittleness, and self-correction — fire continuously. A blueprint with a failing gate does not ship.
