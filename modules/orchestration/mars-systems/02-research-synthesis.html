01Primer

Classify every constraint before you design anything

After the parallel fan-out, MARS does not immediately design. It runs the extracts through an epistemic pass: what do we know, what are we assuming, and which constraints are actually movable. The Epistemic Dimension Playbook is blunt about what kills otherwise-good blueprints — unexamined self-imposed constraints that everyone has treated as hard.

The playbook splits constraints into three bins. Hard constraints are Laws of physics (gravity, entropy), legal requirements, contractual obligations; you Accept and design around. Soft constraints are budget, timeline, staffing — expensive to move, but Negotiate where high ROI. Self-imposed constraints are the dangerous bucket: Assumed to be hard, but changeable. Lines like We've always done it this way or Our customers won't accept that. The prescribed move is Challenge and test assumptions.

The synthesis pass runs four steps in order: (1) Identify all mentioned constraints — list everything blocking the work; (2) Classify each constraint as hard, soft, or self-imposed; (3) Challenge self-imposed constraints — is this actually true, or assumed? Has anyone tested this? (4) Negotiate soft constraints where high ROI.

That sequence is the whole difference between research synthesis and stenography. A specialist returning the regulatory environment makes this impossible is making a claim the synthesizer has to classify — is that a statute, a contract, or a belief? MARS treats the third bucket as the highest-leverage surface area in the whole system, because reclassifying one self-imposed constraint as changeable opens a design space nobody was drawing on yesterday.

Every constraint in your research pile is hard, soft, or self-imposed. The third bucket is the only one where synthesis generates new design space.

### Three Types of Constraints

**HARD CONSTRAINTS** (Cannot change):
```
- Laws of physics (gravity, entropy)
- Legal/regulatory requirements
- Contractual obligations (can't change without cost)
- Natural resource limits
- Market realities (can't force demand)

Approach: Accept and design around
```

**SOFT CONSTRAINTS** (Expensive/difficult to change):
```
- Budget limitations (can raise more, but costs)
- Timeline pressures (can extend, but costs)
- Staff availability (can hire, but costs)
- Technology investments (can upgrade, but costs)
- Organizational culture (can shift, but takes time)

Approach: Negotiate where high ROI
```

**SELF-IMPOSED CONSTRAINTS** (Assumed to be hard, but changeable):
```
- "We've always done it this way"
- "Our customers won't accept that"
- "That's not possible in our industry"
- "We're too small/big to do this"
- "That would never work here"

Approach: Challenge and test assumptions
```
